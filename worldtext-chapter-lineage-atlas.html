Dissertation lineage atlas · working document

Worldtext

Generative AI as a Medium for Verbal and Visual Expression. A generative model does not return only an image or passage, but one temporary surface of a latent world that can be read, written, navigated, sampled, constrained, and contested.

These are constructive lineages: selected intellectual and formal routes that make each chapter defensible. They are not claims that every source was a direct biographical influence.

01 Introduction: The Worldtext ProblemGenerative media turn creative scarcity into interpretive abundance: the problem is no longer only making, but selecting, steering, and accounting for possible worlds. LiteratureR&D +
Question

What is the actual object of creative work when a model can produce more outputs than anyone can inspect?

First conflict

artifact vs. possibility field; production vs. selection; tool vs. medium

Method

medium analysis joined to prototype observation

Lineage chain

  1. Technical reproduction
    Walter Benjamin — The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction

    A production technology changes perception, authority, circulation, and the status of the work—not merely its quantity.

  2. Technical images and apparatus
    Vilém Flusser — Towards a Philosophy of Photography

    The maker works inside a programmed field of possibilities and must learn to play against the apparatus.

  3. Medium conditions
    Marshall McLuhan — Understanding Media; Friedrich Kittler — media materialism

    A medium reorganizes what can be expressed, stored, transmitted, and perceived.

  4. Navigable abundance
    Vannevar Bush — As We May Think; Ted Nelson — hypertext; Jorge Luis Borges — The Library of Babel

    The archive becomes a navigable field whose excess creates a problem of orientation.

  5. Database and generative turn
    Lev Manovich — The Language of New Media; computational creativity and foundation-model practice

    The work shifts from a fixed sequence to a selectable and regenerable space of alternatives.

Migration map

  • reproduction theory→generative medium theory

    The copy is no longer only multiplied; each request can yield a different member of a latent family.

  • content analysis→worldtext analysis

    The visible output is treated as one temporary surface of a larger operational field.

Core citations

Benjamin; Flusser; McLuhan; Kittler; Bush; Nelson; Manovich

Shadow citations

Borges; Umberto Eco’s open work; N. Katherine Hayles on materiality and computation

Adversary

The claim that generative AI is merely a faster neutral tool, or that novelty alone explains its cultural significance.

Evidence stack

interfaces, prompt histories, alternative outputs, selection decisions, failed generations, and the built artifacts that make the medium operable

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Frame the medium through what it permits, withholds, multiplies, and makes difficult. Treat abundance as an operational condition that changes authorship and criticism.
Artifact test
The full dissertation corpus functions as the archive; the introduction establishes why each later artifact is evidence rather than illustration.
Boundary
Worldtext must not become a totalizing metaphor. It names a creative and interpretive condition, not every social, economic, or material fact about AI.

Chapter echo

Describe generative AI from the position of the person forced to choose among possible worlds. Show how interface, model, prompt, and selection jointly define the medium, then state the boundary beyond which worldtext does not explain enough.

02 A Genealogy of the WorldtextWorldtext emerges when signs become simultaneously interpretable, writable, navigable, multimodal, stateful, and generative. Literature +
Question

How does a text become a world without ceasing to be something that must be read?

First conflict

fixed inscription vs. operable field; word vs. image; decoding vs. steering

Method

conceptual genealogy and synthetic distinction-making

Lineage chain

  1. Text as a field of interpretation
    Roland Barthes — From Work to Text; Umberto Eco — The Open Work; Paul Ricoeur — hermeneutics

    A text exceeds authorial intention and is completed through situated acts of interpretation.

  2. Writable and navigable text
    Vannevar Bush — associative trails; Ted Nelson — hypertext; George Landow — hypertext theory

    Digital text can be traversed, linked, reorganized, and partly written through reading.

  3. Word–image inseparability
    G. E. Lessing — Laocoön as the separation to overcome; W. J. T. Mitchell — Picture Theory

    Words and images are not pure media; their meanings emerge through mutual implication and friction.

  4. Meaning through context
    Clifford Geertz — Thick Description

    Interpretation reconstructs the layered situation that makes an act, sign, or output intelligible.

  5. Worlds and traversal
    Nelson Goodman — Ways of Worldmaking; Espen Aarseth — Cybertext; Marie-Laure Ryan — possible-worlds narratology

    A textual system may contain rules, states, paths, and possible worlds that require traversal rather than linear reading.

Migration map

  • text→hypertext

    Reading acquires navigational and configurative action.

  • imagetext→worldtext

    Word–image relation gains state, traversal, and a generative field of unrealized alternatives.

  • interpretation as decoding→interpretation as sampling and steering

    The reader tests the system by changing conditions and comparing resulting worlds.

Core citations

Barthes; Eco; Nelson; Landow; Mitchell; Geertz; Goodman; Aarseth; Ryan

Shadow citations

Ricoeur; Bakhtin’s dialogism; Hayles on electronic textuality

Adversary

Medium purity, the fixed-work model, and the idea that a prompt simply transmits a complete intention to an obedient system.

Evidence stack

conceptual distinctions, interface actions, prompt revisions, generated variants, persistent states, and interpretive reconstructions

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Derive worldtext by adding properties only when an earlier concept cannot carry them. The argument succeeds if each step names a real change in what a reader-maker can do.
Artifact test
Later artifacts serve as retroactive tests: operative description, sampling, state, provenance, and verification each activate one property of the concept.
Boundary
The latent field cannot be fully seen or recovered. Worldtext is inferred through outputs and interactions, not possessed as a complete hidden universe.

Chapter echo

Build the term worldtext through a sequence of necessary inheritances. At every transition, state what the earlier concept explains, what it cannot explain, and which new operation—writing, navigation, multimodality, state, or generation—forces the next term.

03 Method: Thick PromptingPrompting becomes research when the whole run—conditions, interpretations, revisions, failures, and selections—counts as evidence. LiteratureR&D +
Question

How can making with a generative system produce knowledge rather than a polished demonstration?

First conflict

output vs. process; command vs. negotiation; demonstration vs. inquiry

Method

iterative building, controlled variation, situated interpretation, and trace preservation

Lineage chain

  1. Inquiry through consequence
    John Dewey — Logic and Art as Experience

    Knowledge grows through situated action, encountered resistance, and revision rather than detached contemplation alone.

  2. Reflective practice
    Donald Schön — The Reflective Practitioner

    The maker conducts a conversation with materials whose back-talk redirects the design.

  3. Thick interpretation
    Clifford Geertz — Thick Description

    An event becomes intelligible only when its surrounding conditions, distinctions, and stakes are reconstructed.

  4. Situated action
    Lucy Suchman — Plans and Situated Actions

    Plans do not determine action; practical meaning emerges through local interaction with a partially unpredictable system.

  5. Research through design and tracing
    Christopher Frayling; Zimmerman, Forlizzi, and Evenson; Bruno Latour

    Artifacts can generate knowledge when their construction, mediation, and actor relations remain available for analysis.

  6. Experimental software practice
    ablation, property testing, versioning, logging, and iterative prototyping

    A claim becomes stronger when conditions are varied, edge cases are sought, and failures remain inspectable.

Migration map

  • prompt as instruction→prompt as experimental intervention

    Each prompt perturbs a system and reveals how it resolves underspecification.

  • final artifact→documented run

    The evidentiary unit expands to include prompts, states, branches, edits, and rejected outputs.

  • studio intuition→repeatable protocol

    Tacit making is preserved while comparisons and revision rules become explicit.

Core citations

Dewey; Schön; Geertz; Suchman; Research through Design; Latour

Shadow citations

Ethnomethodology; critical making; software testing and notebook science

Adversary

Single-shot prompt spectacle, secret prompt recipes, benchmark-only evaluation, and post hoc claims unsupported by process records.

Evidence stack

prompt versions, model settings, screen recordings, output families, failure cases, decision notes, code, and artifact revisions

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Build a bounded system, vary one meaningful condition, observe what remains and drifts, interpret the resistance, revise the artifact, and preserve the chain.
Artifact test
Every case study uses the method; the chapter supplies a common protocol so later claims can be compared rather than treated as unrelated artworks.
Boundary
No run record exhausts the model’s training history or internal causality. Thick prompting produces situated evidence, not total explanation.

Chapter echo

Write the method as a repeatable cycle of construction, perturbation, comparison, interpretation, and revision. Make failure a first-class result, distinguish observation from inference, and show exactly how an artifact changes because the model resisted intention.

04 Composition: The Writable ImageDescription becomes operative when language does not merely accompany an image but conditions the process that brings it into view. LiteratureCode/Math +
Question

What kind of authorship begins when a sentence can alter objects, space, style, action, and camera at once?

First conflict

description vs. execution; intention vs. interpretation; local plausibility vs. global relation

Method

operative ekphrasis, iterative conditioning, comparative visual analysis

Lineage chain

  1. Ekphrasis
    Homeric shield description; Philostratus; the rhetorical tradition

    Language can construct a scene in the mind by organizing attention, relation, temporality, and affect.

  2. Media separation and contest
    G. E. Lessing — Laocoön

    Word and image are historically assigned different temporal and spatial powers, creating the boundary the chapter tests.

  3. Imagetext and remediation
    W. J. T. Mitchell — Picture Theory; Jay David Bolter and Richard Grusin — Remediation

    Words and images remediate one another; neither is merely an annotation of the other.

  4. Programmed image
    Vilém Flusser — apparatus and technical image; Harun Farocki — operational images

    Images can be produced inside programs and used to act, calculate, or control rather than only represent.

  5. Text-conditioned generation
    contrastive language–image models, diffusion models, latent representations

    A verbal condition reshapes a probability distribution from which a visual result is sampled.

Migration map

  • ekphrasis as verbal representation→operative ekphrasis

    The description becomes part of the causal surface that produces the image.

  • image editing→world specification

    The maker edits relations, implied histories, behavior, and atmosphere through language.

  • direct control→probabilistic conditioning

    Authorship shifts from placing every mark to constraining and evaluating interpretations.

Core citations

ekphrasis; Lessing; Mitchell; Bolter and Grusin; Flusser; Farocki; text-to-image generation

Shadow citations

speech-act theory; conceptual art instructions; Sol LeWitt’s executable descriptions

Adversary

The myth of the perfectly sovereign prompt author and the belief that a plausible image has preserved the described world’s relations.

Evidence stack

prompt variants, image sequences, relation failures, preserved motifs, camera changes, and differences between verbal specification and visual realization

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Treat the prompt as a bundle of constraints whose influence is partial and uneven. Compare which relations survive across samples and which are replaced by learned defaults.
Artifact test
Operative Ekphrasis and Play, Freedom, and AI Films test description as composition across still and moving images.
Boundary
The system often satisfies isolated nouns and styles while losing causal, spatial, or temporal relations. The image can arrive before the intended film exists.

Chapter echo

Compose one scene whose meaning depends on relations rather than objects, then vary the description without changing its core action. Use the resulting failures to define the exact boundary between writing an image and merely requesting one.

05 Assembly Is SamplingWhen footage can be generated without practical limit, editing becomes an alternating process of sampling, evaluating, steering, and resampling. LiteratureCode/MathR&D +
Question

What replaces cutting when the archive expands in response to every editorial decision?

First conflict

finite footage vs. generative abundance; sequence vs. database; fluidity vs. legibility

Method

generative montage, interactive sampling, visible state and provenance

Lineage chain

  1. Montage
    Sergei Eisenstein; Dziga Vertov

    Meaning can arise from collision, interval, rhythm, and the organization of fragments rather than from any single shot.

  2. Reproduction and constellation
    Walter Benjamin — montage method and technical reproducibility

    Fragments become thinkable through new arrangements; the archive can be read by constructing constellations.

  3. Open and recombinant work
    Umberto Eco — The Open Work; sampling and remix cultures

    The work is completed through structured variation, recombination, and selection.

  4. Database cinema
    Lev Manovich — database and narrative

    A collection of possible elements and the sequence drawn from it become distinct layers of the work.

  5. Stochastic search
    Monte Carlo sampling, evolutionary search, recommender loops, interactive machine learning

    A maker explores a distribution through repeated samples and uses evaluation to redirect subsequent search.

Migration map

  • montage of recorded fragments→montage of generated alternatives

    The editor can request material that does not yet exist.

  • archive navigation→distribution steering

    Selection feeds back into the production of the next candidate set.

  • hidden generative pipeline→legible engine

    The system exposes state, lineage, rejection, and consequence while composition continues.

Core citations

Eisenstein; Vertov; Benjamin; Eco; Manovich; sampling and search traditions

Shadow citations

DJ culture; Oulipo; evolutionary art; interactive machine learning

Adversary

The inherited metaphor of editing as subtraction and the opaque generator that hides why one shot follows another.

Evidence stack

candidate pools, selection histories, generation parameters, rejected variants, sequence changes, and the engine’s visible state

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Model assembly as a feedback loop: sample, compare, select, condition, and resample. Legibility requires that each editorial action reveal its consequence without stopping the creative flow.
Artifact test
CINEOSIS and The Machinery of Meaning test abundant footage, fluid assembly, and an engine that can be read while it runs.
Boundary
Abundance can collapse into noise, repetitive defaults, or untraceable automation. A coherent sequence may conceal an incoherent production process.

Chapter echo

Design the editor as a live sampling instrument rather than a bin of clips. Show the current distribution, the selected lineage, and the next consequence with the least possible interface, then test whether viewers can explain how the sequence came to be.

06 The World That RemembersA generated sequence becomes a world only when what has happened constrains what can happen next. LiteratureCode/Math +
Question

Which facts must persist for multiple generations to remain the same world rather than a series of plausible replacements?

First conflict

next-scene plausibility vs. historical continuity; visual similarity vs. causal state

Method

world modeling, state transition design, continuity stress tests

Lineage chain

  1. Worldmaking
    Nelson Goodman — Ways of Worldmaking

    Worlds are constructed through symbol systems, distinctions, rules, and versions rather than simply found intact.

  2. Possible worlds and narrative
    Marie-Laure Ryan — possible-worlds narratology; narratology of character knowledge and causality

    A narrative world contains facts, alternatives, beliefs, and consequences that constrain interpretation.

  3. Ergodic and interactive worlds
    Espen Aarseth — Cybertext; Janet Murray — procedural and participatory environments

    The reader-player traverses a system whose states and rules help produce the experienced sequence.

  4. Game and software state
    finite-state machines, databases, event sourcing, entity-component systems

    Continuity is implemented by representing state explicitly and applying valid transitions rather than regenerating everything from appearance.

  5. Machine memory
    retrieval systems, context windows, external memory, summaries, and agent state

    Generative models can be scaffolded with records, but remembering a fact and using it correctly remain separate problems.

Migration map

  • narrative continuity→formal state

    Characters, objects, relations, and events become explicit variables with transition rules.

  • context repetition→event memory

    The system preserves what happened, not only a description of the current appearance.

  • plausible continuation→consequential continuation

    The next scene must inherit obligations, damage, knowledge, and changed possibilities.

Core citations

Goodman; Ryan; Aarseth; Murray; state machines; event sourcing; external model memory

Shadow citations

Bergson on duration; game save systems; database transaction logs

Adversary

The equation of visual consistency with world continuity and the assumption that a longer prompt automatically creates memory.

Evidence stack

state ledgers, event logs, object identity tests, character knowledge checks, spatial maps, and deliberate contradiction probes

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Separate visible description from authoritative state. Each generation reads a bounded state, proposes changes, and must return a validated transition whose consequences persist.
Artifact test
Coaxing the Ripples, After the Scene, and the LEGOS framework test visual, narrative, social, procedural, and ethical memory.
Boundary
The model tends to rewrite the world at the seam between generations. External memory can preserve facts while the produced scene still ignores their meaning.

Chapter echo

Specify the smallest state ledger that makes a scene consequential. Break it deliberately—remove one fact, alter one identity, erase one obligation—and show which kinds of worldhood disappear first.

07 Legible MechanismsA persuasive generated artifact becomes contestable only when a reader can reconstruct enough of the process, sources, and decisions beneath it. LiteratureR&D +
Question

What must remain traceable for a generated story, simulation, or explanation to be questioned rather than merely believed?

First conflict

story vs. run record; persuasion vs. provenance; explanation vs. total transparency

Method

thick reading, provenance capture, actor and decision tracing

Lineage chain

  1. Interpretive reconstruction
    Clifford Geertz — thick description; Carlo Ginzburg — evidential clues

    Meaning and causality are reconstructed from partial signs by situating them in a larger pattern of action.

  2. Genealogy and archive
    Michel Foucault — archaeology and genealogy; archival provenance traditions

    Claims acquire force through systems of classification, preservation, exclusion, and descent.

  3. Tracing associations
    Bruno Latour — actor-network theory

    An outcome is followed through the heterogeneous people, devices, documents, and translations that made it possible.

  4. Infrastructure and classification
    Geoffrey Bowker and Susan Leigh Star — Sorting Things Out

    Categories and records are material infrastructures whose histories shape what can be seen and acted upon.

  5. Computational provenance and documentation
    data lineage; reproducible workflows; model cards; datasheets for datasets

    Technical records can expose sources, transformations, limits, and intended uses without pretending to reveal every internal cause.

Migration map

  • close reading of the output→thick reading of the production ecology

    Interpretation expands to prompts, agents, sources, code, interfaces, and selections.

  • authorship attribution→provenance graph

    A single author label is replaced by a recoverable sequence of contributions and transformations.

  • transparency→contestability

    The goal becomes providing practical points where a claim can be checked, challenged, or revised.

Core citations

Geertz; Ginzburg; Foucault; Latour; Bowker and Star; data provenance; model documentation

Shadow citations

archival diplomatics; reproducible science; critical code studies

Adversary

The belief that a fluent output explains itself, and the opposite fantasy that complete transparency into a model is possible.

Evidence stack

source links, prompt and agent traces, transformation logs, version histories, decision annotations, and discrepancies between narrative and run record

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Capture only provenance that changes interpretation or permits challenge. Preserve uncertainty and distinguish observed records from inferred causal stories.
Artifact test
Centaur Box and Growing Entanglements expose how history, agent decisions, and human interventions enter a persuasive result.
Boundary
A record can be complete yet meaningless, while a compelling story can remain underdocumented. Provenance supports judgment but cannot automate it.

Chapter echo

Choose one persuasive output and reconstruct the smallest evidentiary chain needed to challenge it. Make every transformation inspectable, mark every inference, and preserve the gap between what the run record proves and what the story claims.

08 Legal WorldsGeneration becomes construction when every proposed object must obey explicit rules and survive independent verification. Code/MathR&D +
Question

How can an open-ended model produce structures that are not only plausible but valid, reproducible, and buildable?

First conflict

appearance vs. validity; freedom vs. constraint; generation vs. verification

Method

formal representation, constraint checking, generate–validate–repair loops

Lineage chain

  1. Axiomatic construction
    Euclidean geometry and formal systems

    A world becomes checkable when primitive objects, permitted operations, and valid relations are explicitly defined.

  2. Modular construction
    standardized parts, LEGO’s system of play, and Seymour Papert’s constructionism

    Creative freedom can emerge from a finite vocabulary of interoperable elements and visible rules.

  3. Computer-aided design
    solid modeling, coordinate systems, collision tests, and assembly constraints

    A representation must encode geometry and connection, not only a surface image.

  4. Constraint computation
    constraint-satisfaction problems, SAT/SMT solving, graph validation

    A candidate structure is accepted only when all declared invariants hold.

  5. Executable specification
    type systems, formal verification, property-based testing

    A claim is strengthened when invalid states are difficult or impossible to represent and counterexamples are actively sought.

  6. Machine-readable construction
    LDraw and structured part libraries

    A generated design can be parsed, checked, repaired, rendered, and physically reproduced from the same description.

Migration map

  • image prompt→typed construction specification

    Objects become named parts with coordinates, orientations, and legal connections.

  • one-shot generation→generate–validate–repair

    The model proposes; deterministic systems reject or diagnose; the model revises.

  • aesthetic judgment→dual evaluation

    A structure must be both expressive and formally valid.

Core citations

formal systems; modular construction; CAD; CSP/SAT; type systems; property testing; LDraw

Shadow citations

Papert; pattern languages; procedural content generation with constraints

Adversary

Visual plausibility as a substitute for truth, and unrestricted generation that leaves verification to a human after the fact.

Evidence stack

part inventories, connection graphs, collision reports, support checks, parser errors, repair histories, and successful reconstruction

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Define primitives and invariants first. Permit the model to generate only a structured proposal, validate it deterministically, return localized errors, and preserve the repaired lineage.
Artifact test
Can a Model Build with LEGO? uses LDraw to test whether an attractive output is composed of real parts in legal, reproducible relations.
Boundary
Formal validity does not guarantee usefulness, beauty, safety, or physical stability beyond the rules represented. Every legal world is only as adequate as its constraints.

Chapter echo

Build a generator whose outputs can fail loudly and locally. Make invalidity visible at the exact connection where it occurs, then compare unconstrained plausibility with verified construction to show what formal discipline adds and what it still omits.

09 Misreading as MaterialA classifier’s confident mistake becomes critical material when its assumptions, uncertainty, and consequences are made tangible. LiteratureR&D +
Question

What does an automated system claim to know when it converts a person’s expression into a stable emotional label?

First conflict

signal vs. interpretation; confidence vs. validity; classification vs. lived context

Method

subgroup auditing, critical interface construction, materialization of labels

Lineage chain

  1. Measurement of expression
    Charles Darwin — The Expression of the Emotions; histories of physiognomy and biometric classification

    Visible bodies have repeatedly been treated as readable evidence of hidden character or inner state.

  2. Basic-emotion classification
    Paul Ekman and automated facial-expression traditions

    A small set of emotion categories is treated as universally detectable from observable configurations.

  3. Constructed and contextual emotion
    Lisa Feldman Barrett; Alan Fridlund

    Emotion is not a simple substance written on the face; context, concepts, social action, and interpretation are constitutive.

  4. Classification infrastructures
    Geoffrey Bowker and Susan Leigh Star

    Categories become consequential when institutions embed them in workflows, records, and decisions.

  5. Critical algorithm studies
    Safiya Umoja Noble; Ruha Benjamin; Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru

    Technical error is unevenly distributed and can reproduce historical power while appearing objective.

  6. Critical and adversarial design
    Dunne and Raby; Carl DiSalvo

    Artifacts can stage disagreement and make a system’s assumptions available for public encounter.

Migration map

  • emotion detection→institutional interpretation

    The label is followed into the decisions and expectations it authorizes.

  • accuracy score→situated audit

    Performance is separated by subgroup, context, uncertainty, and consequence.

  • hidden classification→material consequence

    The label produces a visible object or event that can be challenged.

Core citations

Darwin; Ekman as a contested lineage; Barrett; Fridlund; Bowker and Star; Noble; Benjamin; Buolamwini and Gebru; critical design

Shadow citations

history of physiognomy; feminist standpoint theory; disability studies critiques of normative expression

Adversary

The claim that emotion is a universal visible fact and that bias is merely a removable edge case in an otherwise neutral classifier.

Evidence stack

confusion matrices, calibration, subgroup performance, contradictory labels, participant interpretation, interface behavior, and the physical output tied to a prediction

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Do not use error only as a metric. Stage the exact movement from signal to category to consequence so that the system’s confidence and the world’s ambiguity collide in public.
Artifact test
Auditing Emotion AI and the Gumball Emotion Machine turn uncertain classifications into inspectable and consequential objects.
Boundary
Exposure does not by itself change the institution that purchases or operationalizes the classifier. A critical artifact can reveal a relation without dissolving it.

Chapter echo

Select one label the system treats as obvious. Reconstruct the assumptions required to produce it, audit who is misread and under what conditions, then materialize the prediction so its uncertainty can no longer disappear behind a score.

10 Where Responsibility GoesGenerative systems distribute creative action across many actors, but responsibility becomes visible only when praise, harm, dependence, or grief demands an account. LiteratureR&D +
Question

Who becomes answerable for an outcome assembled from prompting, generation, selection, deployment, and interpretation?

First conflict

distributed agency vs. displaced accountability; authorship vs. consequence

Method

actor mapping, responsibility decomposition, staged encounters and pronoun analysis

Lineage chain

  1. The unstable author
    Roland Barthes — The Death of the Author; Michel Foucault — What Is an Author?

    Authorship is an institutional function that organizes interpretation, ownership, and authority rather than a simple origin point.

  2. Distributed action
    Bruno Latour; Madeleine Akrich; Lucy Suchman

    Agency emerges through relations among people, artifacts, scripts, plans, institutions, and situated adjustments.

  3. Responsibility gaps
    Andreas Matthias — responsibility gap; Luciano Floridi — distributed morality

    Learning and distributed systems complicate the assignment of responsibility without eliminating moral consequence.

  4. Moral crumple zones
    Madeleine Clare Elish

    Humans may absorb blame for failures of systems they cannot fully control while institutions retain the benefits.

  5. Relational machines
    Sherry Turkle and work on relational artifacts

    People can experience attachment, care, projection, and loss around systems whose reciprocity is asymmetrical.

  6. Accountable systems
    audit trails, human-in-the-loop governance, responsibility matrices

    Operational systems can preserve handoffs, authority, overrides, and foreseeable consequences.

Migration map

  • single author→agency stack

    Initiating, generative, selective, interpretive, institutional, and consequential agency are separated.

  • who caused it?→who could know, alter, authorize, benefit, or repair?

    Responsibility is mapped through capacities and obligations rather than a single causal label.

  • I made it / it made it→pronoun alibi

    Language is analyzed as a device that moves credit and blame across the seam.

Core citations

Barthes; Foucault; Latour; Akrich; Suchman; Matthias; Floridi; Elish; Turkle

Shadow citations

care ethics; legal chain-of-responsibility models; Nissenbaum on accountability and contextual integrity

Adversary

Both machine autonomy that absolves institutions and human-only authorship that ignores system behavior, infrastructure, and delegated decisions.

Evidence stack

prompt and selection histories, publication decisions, interface defaults, override points, pronoun shifts, participant testimony, and records of who bore the consequence

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
Map agency before assigning responsibility. For each actor, record knowledge, control, benefit, exposure, duty, and capacity to repair; then show where the account becomes evasive.
Artifact test
The Pronoun Alibi and The Adviser Leaves the Room stage the movement of credit, liability, attachment, and grief across the human–model seam.
Boundary
A precise map does not automatically settle legal or moral responsibility. The chapter reveals where accountability is displaced and which obligations remain contestable.

Chapter echo

Begin after the outcome, when someone must explain what happened. Follow every shift between I, we, it, and the system; map who initiated, selected, authorized, benefited, and suffered; then design the artifact so no participant can claim agency without also encountering its corresponding obligation.

11 Worldtext as MediumWorldtext is useful only if it keeps expressive possibility, operational structure, critical inspection, and material limits visible at the same time. LiteratureR&DCode/Math +
Question

What does worldtext explain across the artifacts, and where must the concept stop?

First conflict

synthesis vs. totalization; possibility vs. constraint; expression vs. accountability

Method

cross-case synthesis, boundary testing, design principles, and research-program construction

Lineage chain

  1. Medium theory
    Marshall McLuhan; Vilém Flusser; Friedrich Kittler

    Media are environments and programmed conditions that reorganize perception, action, storage, and cultural form.

  2. Affordances
    James J. Gibson; Donald Norman

    A medium offers action possibilities that arise through relations among capacities, environments, conventions, and interfaces.

  3. Worldmaking
    Nelson Goodman

    Worlds are made through symbol systems, classifications, versions, and operations whose adequacy can be compared.

  4. Pragmatist inquiry and aesthetics
    John Dewey

    Meaning and value arise through experience, consequence, experimentation, and reconstruction.

  5. Situated and critical making
    Donna Haraway — situated knowledges; Matt Ratto — critical making; Susan Leigh Star — boundary objects

    Knowledge is partial and embodied; building can expose relations; concepts can coordinate inquiry without erasing difference.

  6. Responsible innovation
    anticipation, reflexivity, inclusion, responsiveness, and sociotechnical evaluation

    A research program must state who benefits, what is excluded, how harms are monitored, and when a line of development should change.

Migration map

  • individual chapter claims→affordance–constraint matrix

    Each artifact contributes one capacity, one failure mode, and one design obligation.

  • worldtext as description→worldtext as research program

    The concept becomes a guide for building, evaluating, and criticizing future generative systems.

  • medium interior→material and institutional exterior

    Energy, water, labor, access, policy, and political economy are marked as conditions the frame cannot metabolize alone.

Core citations

McLuhan; Flusser; Kittler; Gibson; Norman; Goodman; Dewey; Haraway; Ratto; Star; responsible innovation

Shadow citations

Simondon on technical individuation; Eco’s open work; design justice

Adversary

A universal theory of AI, a booster-versus-doom binary, and the assumption that an expressive artifact automatically resolves the system’s external costs.

Evidence stack

the cross-case record of affordances, failures, state models, provenance, validations, audits, responsibility maps, and explicitly unaddressed conditions

Signature chapter ecology

Construction logic
For every claimed affordance, name the artifact that demonstrates it, the failure that limits it, the evidence required to judge it, and the design obligation that follows.
Artifact test
All case studies are recomposed as a comparative medium map rather than summarized as a portfolio.
Boundary
Worldtext can make meaning, interaction, state, evidence, and agency inspectable; it does not by itself render compute supply chains, labor, water, energy, ownership, or geopolitical power adequately visible.
